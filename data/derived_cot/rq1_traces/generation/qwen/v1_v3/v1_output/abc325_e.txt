The solution involves calculating the shortest path for each mode of transportation separately and then combining them to find the optimal switching point.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The problem involves navigating a graph where each edge (between cities) can be traversed either by car or train. The key constraint is that after switching from car to train, all subsequent travel must be by train. The goal is to find the minimum travel time from the starting city to the destination city, considering all possible switching points.

2. **Intuition**: The solution can be broken down into two parts:
   - **Car-only Journey**: Calculate the shortest path from the starting city to every other city using only the car.
   - **Train-only Journey**: Calculate the shortest path from every city to the destination city using only the train.
   The optimal solution will be the minimum sum of the car-only path to a city and the train-only path from that city to the destination.

3. **Algorithm Selection**: Dijkstra's algorithm is used for both the car and train graphs because it efficiently finds the shortest paths in a graph with non-negative edge weights. The car graph is constructed with edge weights as `D[i][j] * A`, and the train graph with weights `D[i][j] * B + C`.

4. **Complexity Analysis**: Dijkstra's algorithm runs in O(E log V) time for each graph, where E is the number of edges and V is the number of vertices. Since the graph is complete (each city is connected to every other city), E is O(V^2). For N cities, the total complexity is O(N^2 log N), which is feasible for N up to 1000.
