=== ANSWER ===

To solve this problem, we need to determine whether the length of the line segment connecting two points in a regular pentagon is the same as the length of another line segment connecting two different points. The key observation here is that in a regular pentagon, all sides are of equal length, and all diagonals are of equal length. Therefore, the problem reduces to checking whether both segments are either sides or diagonals of the pentagon.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The pentagon has vertices labeled A, B, C, D, and E in cyclic order. The length of a segment depends on the circular distance between the two vertices it connects. 
2. **Key Insight**: The circular distance between two vertices in a pentagon can be either 1 (indicating a side) or 2 (indicating a diagonal). All segments with a circular distance of 1 are sides and thus equal in length, while all segments with a circular distance of 2 are diagonals and equal in length.
3. **Algorithm Selection**: For each segment (given as two characters), compute the circular distance between the corresponding vertices. If both segments have the same circular distance (both 1 or both 2), their lengths are equal; otherwise, they are not.
4. **Complexity Analysis**: The solution involves simple arithmetic operations and comparisons, making it efficient with constant time complexity O(1).

### Solution Code
```python
mapping = {'A': 0, 'B': 1, 'C': 2, 'D': 3, 'E': 4}

s_line = input().strip()
t_line = input().strip()

def get_distance(s):
    p1 = mapping[s[0]]
    p2 = mapping[s[1]]
    diff = abs(p1 - p2)
    return min(diff, 5 - diff)

d1 = get_distance(s_line)
d2 = get_distance(t_line)

if d1 == d2:
    print("Yes")
else:
    print("No")
```

### Explanation
1. **Mapping Vertices**: The vertices A, B, C, D, and E are mapped to the numbers 0 through 4 for easier computation.
2. **Distance Calculation**: For each segment (two characters), the function `get_distance` calculates the circular distance between the two vertices. This is done by finding the absolute difference in their mapped values and then taking the minimum of this difference and 5 minus the difference (due to the cyclic nature of the pentagon).
3. **Comparison**: The circular distances of the two segments are compared. If they are the same (both 1 or both 2), the output is "Yes"; otherwise, it is "No".

This approach efficiently checks the equality of segment lengths by leveraging the geometric properties of a regular pentagon, ensuring correctness with minimal computational overhead.
