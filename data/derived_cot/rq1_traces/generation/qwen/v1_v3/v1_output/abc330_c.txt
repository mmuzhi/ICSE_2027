To solve this problem, we need to find the minimum absolute difference between the sum of squares of two non-negative integers and a given positive integer \( D \). The solution involves efficiently checking possible pairs of integers \( (x, y) \) such that \( x^2 + y^2 \) is as close as possible to \( D \).

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The task is to minimize \( |x^2 + y^2 - D| \) for non-negative integers \( x \) and \( y \). Given the constraints \( 1 \leq D \leq 2 \times 10^{12} \), a brute-force approach is infeasible due to the large possible range of \( x \) and \( y \).

2. **Key Insight**: For each \( x \) from 0 to \( \sqrt{D} \), compute the remaining value \( \text{rem} = D - x^2 \). The optimal \( y \) for this \( x \) will be either the floor or the ceiling of \( \sqrt{\text{rem}} \), as these are the closest integers to the actual square root of \( \text{rem} \).

3. **Algorithm Selection**: 
   - Iterate \( x \) from 0 to \( \lfloor \sqrt{D} \rfloor \).
   - For each \( x \), compute \( \text{rem} = D - x^2 \).
   - Find \( y0 = \lfloor \sqrt{\text{rem}} \rfloor \) and consider \( y = y0 \) and \( y = y0 + 1 \).
   - Track the minimal absolute difference encountered during these checks.
   - Additionally, consider the smallest integer \( x \) greater than \( \sqrt{D} \) (i.e., \( x = \lfloor \sqrt{D} \rfloor + 1 \)) and \( y = 0 \), as this might yield a smaller absolute difference.

4. **Complexity Analysis**: The algorithm runs in \( O(\sqrt{D}) \) time, which is feasible given the constraint \( D \leq 2 \times 10^{12} \) (since \( \sqrt{2 \times 10^{12}} \approx 1.4 \times 10^6 \)).
