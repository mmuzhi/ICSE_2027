To solve this problem, we need to compute the expected value of the inversion number of a permutation after performing a specific operation. The operation involves selecting a contiguous subarray of length K uniformly at random and shuffling it. The inversion number is the count of pairs (i, j) where i < j and the element at position i is greater than the element at position j.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The key observation is that the expected inversion number can be derived by considering each pair (i, j) in the permutation and calculating the probability that they form an inversion after the operation. The operation involves selecting a segment of length K and shuffling it, which affects the inversion status of pairs within and across the segment.

2. **Intuition**: For each segment of length K, the expected inversion number after shuffling can be broken down into three parts:
   - **Within the segment**: The expected inversion number is K*(K-1)/4 because any two elements in a random permutation are equally likely to be in either order.
   - **Outside the segment**: The inversion number remains unchanged since these elements are not shuffled.
   - **Crossing the segment**: The probability that a pair (i, j) with one element in the segment and the other outside forms an inversion depends on the values in the segment and the fixed value outside.

3. **Algorithm Selection**: For each possible segment, compute the expected inversion number by:
   - Calculating the expected inversions within the segment.
   - Calculating the fixed inversions outside the segment.
   - Calculating the expected inversions between the segment and the outside elements.
   Sum these values for all segments and average them to get the final expected inversion number.

4. **Complexity Analysis**: The algorithm involves iterating over each segment (O(N) segments) and for each segment, processing all elements (O(N) operations). Thus, the overall complexity is O(N^2), which is feasible for small N (up to 1000) but may not scale for larger N.
