The sequence repeats every N elements, and the challenge is to efficiently check for the existence of such a subsequence without explicitly constructing the infinite sequence.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The problem involves checking for a contiguous subsequence in an infinite periodic sequence that sums to S. The sequence repeats every N elements, so any subsequence can be broken down into full periods and a remaining segment. The key insight is that any subsequence sum can be expressed as `k * T + X`, where `T` is the sum of one period, `k` is the number of full periods, and `X` is the sum of the remaining segment (which must be between 1 and T).

2. **Handling Large S**: If S is very large (greater than 2×10¹⁴), we leverage the periodicity by checking if S is a multiple of T (i.e., `S % T == 0`). If it is, the answer is "Yes" because the entire sequence can be divided into full periods summing to S. Otherwise, we check if the remainder `S % T` can be formed by a contiguous segment in the circular array.

3. **Handling Small S**: For S ≤ 2×10¹⁴, we determine the largest integer `k` such that `k * T ≤ S - 1`. The remaining value `X = S - k * T` must be between 1 and T and must be a sum of some contiguous segment in the circular array (two periods of the original sequence). We use a sliding window approach with a dictionary to efficiently check for the presence of `X` in the contiguous segments of the circular array.
