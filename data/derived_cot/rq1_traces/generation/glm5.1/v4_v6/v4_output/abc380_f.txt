=== ANSWER ===
This problem can be solved using game theory with state-space search and memoization. 

### Approach
1. **State Representation**: The total number of cards is at most 12. We can represent the state of the game by tracking the location of each card: either in Takahashi's hand, in Aoki's hand, or on the table. Since there are 3 possible locations for each of the up to 12 cards, we can encode the state into a base-3 integer. We also need to track whose turn it is. Thus, a state is completely defined by `(mask, turn)`.
2. **Game Transitions**: From a given state, the current player must play a card from their hand. For each card they can play:
    - They place it on the table.
    - They may choose to take one card from the table that has a strictly smaller value, or take no card at all.
    - This generates new states. If any of these new states leads to a loss for the opponent, the current player can force a win from the current state.
3. **Base Case**: If the current player has no cards in their hand, they cannot make a move and lose.
4. **Memoization**: We can use memoization (DP) to avoid recomputing states. The maximum number of states is $3^{12} \times 2 \approx 10^6$, which easily fits in memory and time limits.
5. **Game Finite Guarantee**: The sum of the values of the cards in both players' hands strictly decreases with every move (since a player either takes a smaller card or takes nothing). This ensures the game cannot cycle and will always terminate, so our DFS will not encounter infinite loops.
