You will be given a competitive programming problem.
Takahashi decided to hold a programming contest.
The contest consists of five problems: A, B, C, D, E, with scores a, b, c, d, e, respectively.
There are 31 participants, and all of them solved at least one problem.
More specifically, for every non-empty subsequence (not necessarily contiguous) of the string ABCDE, there is a participant named after that subsequence who solved the problems corresponding to the letters in their name and did not solve the other problems.
For example, participant A solved only problem A, and participant BCE solved problems B, C, and E.
Print the names of the participants in order of their obtained scores, from the largest to the smallest.
The score obtained by a participant is the sum of the scores of the problems they solved.
If two participants obtained the same score, print the one whose name is lexicographically smaller first.
What does "lexicographically smaller" mean?
In short, "lexicographically smaller" refers to the order in which words would appear in a dictionary.
More precisely, for distinct strings S,T consisting of uppercase English letters, S is lexicographically smaller than T if either of the following conditions holds:

- The length |S| of S is less than the length of T, and the first |S| characters of T match S.
- There exists an integer 1\leq i\leq\min\{ |S|,|T|\} that satisfy both of the following two conditions:
- For every integer j with 1\leq j\lt i, the j-th character of S equals the j-th character of T.
- The i-th character of S is alphabetically smaller than the i-th character of T.


For example, if S= AB and T= ABC, the first condition holds, so S is lexicographically smaller than T.
If S= ABD and T= ACD, the second condition holds for i=2, so S is lexicographically smaller than T.

=== ANSWER ===
To solve this problem, we need to generate the names of all participants in a programming contest, where each participant solved a distinct non-empty subsequence of problems A, B, C, D, and E. The participants must be sorted in descending order of their total scores, which is the sum of the scores of the problems they solved. If two participants have the same score, they should be sorted lexicographically in ascending order based on their names.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The contest involves five problems (A, B, C, D, E) with respective scores. There are 31 participants, each corresponding to a non-empty subsequence of the problems. Each participant's score is the sum of the scores of the problems they solved.
2. **Key Insight**: The participants' names are derived by taking subsequences of "ABCDE" in the order the letters appear. For example, the subsequence "BCE" includes problems B, C, and E in that order.
3. **Algorithm Selection**: We use bitmasking to generate all 31 non-empty subsequences efficiently. Each bitmask represents a unique combination of problems (from 1 to 31 in binary). For each bitmask, we construct the participant's name and calculate their total score.
4. **Sorting**: Participants are sorted primarily by their scores in descending order. If scores are equal, participants are sorted lexicographically by their names in ascending order (dictionary order).

### Solution Code
```python
def main():
    a, b, c, d, e = map(int, input().split())
    scores_list = [a, b, c, d, e]
    s = "ABCDE"
    participants = []
    for mask in range(1, 32):
        name = ""
        total_score = 0
        for i in range(5):
            if mask & (1 << i):
                name += s[i]
                total_score += scores_list[i]
        participants.append((name, total_score))
    
    sorted_participants = sorted(participants, key=lambda x: (-x[1], x[0]))
    
    for name, _ in sorted_participants:
        print(name)

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
```

### Explanation
1. **Reading Input**: The scores for problems A, B, C, D, and E are read from the input.
2. **Generating Participants**: For each bitmask from 1 to 31 (representing all non-empty subsets of the five problems), the corresponding participant's name is constructed by including the letters from "ABCDE" where the bitmask has a set bit. The total score is computed by summing the scores of these problems.
3. **Sorting Participants**: The list of participants is sorted using a custom key. The primary key is the negative of the score to achieve descending order. The secondary key is the name itself to ensure lexicographical order in case of tied scores.
4. **Output**: The sorted list of participant names is printed, one per line, in the required order.

This approach efficiently handles the generation and sorting of participants, ensuring the output meets the specified criteria of descending scores and lexicographical order for tied scores.
