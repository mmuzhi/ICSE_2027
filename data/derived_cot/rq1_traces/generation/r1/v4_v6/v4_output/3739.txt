To solve this problem, we need to compute the sum of Manhattan distances between every pair of identical pieces over all valid arrangements on an m x n grid. The key insight is recognizing that the Manhattan distance can be decomposed into row and column differences, which can be computed independently. 

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The problem involves placing k identical pieces on an m x n grid such that no two pieces share the same cell. For each valid arrangement, we need to sum the Manhattan distances between all pairs of pieces. The Manhattan distance between two cells (x1, y1) and (x2, y2) is |x1 - x2| + |y1 - y2|. Given the constraints (m and n can be up to 10^5, but m*n ≤ 10^5), a brute-force approach is infeasible.
2. **Key Insight**: The sum of Manhattan distances over all arrangements can be separated into the sum of row differences and the sum of column differences. For each pair of distinct cells, the row difference and column difference contribute independently to the total distance.
3. **Mathematical Formulation**:
   - **Row Contribution**: For all unordered pairs of distinct rows (i, j), the row difference |i - j| is multiplied by the number of ways to choose any two columns (n²) and the number of arrangements that include both cells (C(m*n - 2, k - 2)).
   - **Column Contribution**: Similarly, for all unordered pairs of distinct columns (i, j), the column difference |i - j| is multiplied by the number of ways to choose any two rows (m²) and the same arrangement count.
4. **Efficient Calculation**: 
   - The sum of absolute differences for all row pairs (0 to m-1) is computed as m*(m-1)*(m+1)//6. Similarly for columns, it's n*(n-1)*(n+1)//6.
   - Precompute factorials and their modular inverses up to 100,000 to efficiently compute combinations modulo 10^9 + 7.
5. **Combination**: The total sum is (row_contribution + column_contribution) * C(m*n - 2, k - 2) modulo 10^9 + 7.
