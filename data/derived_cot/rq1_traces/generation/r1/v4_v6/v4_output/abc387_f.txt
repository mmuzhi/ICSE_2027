The solution involves analyzing the functional graph defined by the sequence \( A \) to decompose it into cycles and trees, then using dynamic programming to count valid assignments efficiently.

### Approach
1. **Graph Decomposition**: The sequence \( A \) defines a directed graph where each node \( i \) points to \( A_i \). This graph decomposes into cycles and trees (directed towards the cycles). Nodes not part of any cycle form trees rooted at cycle nodes.
2. **Cycle and Tree Processing**: For each cycle, all nodes in the cycle must have the same value \( v \) (from 1 to \( M \)). For tree nodes, the value must be at most \( v \) and satisfy \( x_i \leq x_{A_i} \).
3. **Dynamic Programming (DP)**: For each tree node, compute the number of valid assignments where the node's value is at most \( v \). The DP state \( G[u][v] \) represents the number of valid assignments for the subtree rooted at \( u \) when \( u \)'s value is at most \( v \).
4. **Component-wise Calculation**: For each cycle component, the total valid assignments are the sum over \( v \) (from 1 to \( M \)) of the product of valid assignments for each tree attached to the cycle nodes. The final answer is the product of these sums across all components.
