About bitwise XOR
For non-negative integers a and b, the bitwise XOR a \oplus b is defined as follows:


In the binary representation of a \oplus b, the digit in the 2^k place (k \ge 0) is 1 if and only if exactly one of the digits in the 2^k place of a and b is 1; otherwise, it is 0.


For example, 3 \oplus 5 = 6 (in binary, 011 \oplus 101 = 110).
In general, for k non-negative integers x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k, their bitwise XOR x_1 \oplus x_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus x_k is defined as (\cdots((x_1 \oplus x_2) \oplus x_3) \oplus \cdots) \oplus x_k, which does not depend on the order of x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k.

It can be proved that under the constraints of this problem, the number of possible values is finite.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The problem involves partitioning the initial bags into up to `n` groups (some groups may be empty) and computing the XOR of the sums of stones in each group. The challenge is to count all distinct XOR values obtainable through any such partition.
2. **Intuition**: For small values of `n` (≤ 9), we can directly iterate over all possible assignments of bags to bins (groups) and compute the distinct XOR values. 3. **Algorithm Selection**:

   - **Meet-in-the-Middle (n = 10)**: Split the bags into two halves. For each half, compute all possible bin contents and their XOR values. Then, for each combination of partial XOR values from both halves, compute the final XOR and store it in a set.
