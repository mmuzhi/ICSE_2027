To solve this problem, we need to determine the number of integers \( x \) such that the three integers \( A \), \( B \), and \( x \) can be arranged to form an arithmetic sequence. An arithmetic sequence has the property that the difference between consecutive terms is constant. 

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The key observation is that for three numbers to form an arithmetic sequence, one of them must be the average of the other two. This leads us to three possible cases:
   - \( x \) is the average of \( A \) and \( B \), i.e., \( x = (A + B) / 2 \). This is only valid if \( A + B \) is even.
   - \( x \) is derived from \( A \) and \( B \) such that \( 2B = A + x \), i.e., \( x = 2B - A \).
   - \( x \) is derived from \( A \) and \( B \) such that \( 2A = B + x \), i.e., \( x = 2A - B \).

2. **Algorithm Selection**: We generate all possible candidate values for \( x \) based on the above three cases. We then count the distinct integer values among these candidates. The solution involves checking these three conditions and collecting the valid \( x \) values in a set to avoid duplicates.

3. **Complexity Analysis**: The algorithm involves a constant number of arithmetic operations and set operations, making it efficient with a time complexity of \( O(1) \). The space complexity is also \( O(1) \) since we only store up to three distinct values.
