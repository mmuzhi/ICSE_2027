To solve this problem, we need to determine the sum of the smallest non-negative integers \( x \) for all pairs of sequences \( (i, j) \) where \( i \leq j \), such that after performing the given operation \( x \) times, the sequences \( A_i \) and \( A_j \) become identical. The operation involves replacing each element \( A_{i,k} \) with the sum of the first \( k \) elements modulo 2.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The key observation is that the operation is linear and can be represented as a transformation matrix in GF(2). The transformation applied \( x \) times is \( T^x \), and we need to find the smallest \( x \) such that \( T^x \cdot A_i = T^x \cdot A_j \). This is equivalent to \( T^x \cdot (A_i + A_j) = 0 \).

2. **Insight**: The transformation \( T \) is nilpotent, meaning \( T^x \) eventually becomes zero for sufficiently large \( x \). However, the problem requires the smallest \( x \) for each pair \( (i, j) \). Given the constraints, a brute-force approach is infeasible for large inputs.

3. **Algorithm Selection**: For each pair \( (i, j) \), we simulate the transformation until the sequences match or a cycle is detected. The simulation involves applying the operation \( x \) times and checking if the sequences are identical. The minimal \( x \) is recorded for each pair.

4. **Complexity Analysis**: The worst-case complexity is \( O(N^2 \cdot M) \), where \( N \) is the number of sequences and \( M \) is the length of each sequence. Given the constraint \( N \times M \leq 10^6 \), this approach may be feasible for small inputs but could be inefficient for larger ones. However, optimizations like cycle detection can help reduce the average-case complexity.
