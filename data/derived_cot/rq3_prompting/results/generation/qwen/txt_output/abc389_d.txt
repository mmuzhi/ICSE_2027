The squares are defined by their integer coordinates (i, j), and a square is considered contained if all four of its corners are within or on the circle.

### Approach
1. **Problem Analysis**: The key observation is that a square with center (i, j) is entirely contained within the circle if the distance from the origin to the farthest corner of the square is less than or equal to R. The farthest corner from the origin in the square is one of the four corners: (i±0.5, j±0.5). The condition for the square to be inside the circle is that the distance from the origin to any of these corners must be ≤ R.

2. **Mathematical Insight**: The condition for the square (i, j) to be inside the circle can be simplified using the geometric property that the entire square is contained if the distance from the origin to the center of the square plus the maximum distance from the center to any corner (which is 0.5√2) is ≤ R. This translates to the condition \(i^2 + j^2 \leq (R - 0.5\sqrt{2})^2\).

3. **Algorithm Selection**: We iterate over all integer coordinates (i, j) within the range determined by the circle's radius (adjusted by 0.5√2). For each i, we determine the range of j values that satisfy the condition \(i^2 + j^2 \leq (R - 0.5\sqrt{2})^2\) using binary search to efficiently find the maximum j for each i.

4. **Complexity Analysis**: The algorithm involves iterating over approximately \(2R\) values of i (since i ranges from \(-\text{floor}(R - 0.5\sqrt{2})\) to \(\text{floor}(R - 0.5\sqrt{2})\)), and for each i, performing a binary search to find the valid j values. The binary search operates in \(O(\log(\text{max\_j}))\) time, where max_j is up to \(10^6\) for \(R = 10^6\). Thus, the total complexity is \(O(R \log R)\), which is efficient for the given constraints.
