Now we need to consider potential pitfalls:

- The graph may have multiple edges between same vertices. Our Floyd-Warshall should take the minimum weight for each pair when initializing. Since we have multiple edges, we should set d[u][v] = min(d[u][v], w) for each edge. Floyd will then compute shortest paths considering all edges.

- The required edges themselves are part of the graph, and they may be the only connection between some vertices. Our distances d include them, which is fine.

- In the single-edge formula, we used dist_imp[a][b] which is the shortest distance between a and b. This may be less than w if there is a shorter alternative path. That's accounted for in cost3/cost4.

- For masks with multiple edges, the DP over subsets should be correct. But we must ensure that we consider all partitions, including those where the two subsets are not necessarily covered in sequence without overlap? The recurrence covers all splits. However, is it possible that the optimal walk cannot be split into two parts where the first part covers a subset S and ends at some vertex x, and the second part covers the rest, without the second part needing to start at x at the exact moment the first part ends? In a continuous walk, after covering S, we are at some vertex x. Then we continue to cover the rest. So yes, it can be split.

- But what about the possibility that the optimal walk covers some edges, then later covers some edges, but also revisits some edges from S during the second part in a way that reduces cost compared to covering S first optimally and then covering the rest optimally separately? The separate optimal for S might be different from the prefix of the optimal combined walk. However, the recurrence considers the minimum over x and over S of g[S][i][x] + g[T\S][x][j]. This allows the prefix to be any optimal way to cover S ending at x, and the suffix any optimal way to cover the rest starting at x. If the combined optimal walk has a prefix that is not optimal for covering S alone (because it takes extra detours to facilitate the suffix), then the sum of the optimal separate solutions might be higher than the combined optimal. But we are taking the minimum over x and S, and the prefix in the combined optimal walk is a particular way to cover S ending at x, whose cost is at least g[S][i][x] (since g is the minimum).
