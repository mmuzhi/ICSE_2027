Looking at this problem, I need to understand what happens when we reverse a valid parenthesis substring with flipping.

Key observations:
1. When we reverse a valid substring with flipping, we're essentially "mirroring" that subtree structure
2. The operation can rearrange the order of children at any node in the parse tree
3. Two strings are reachable from each other if and only if they have the same "unordered tree structure" (same structure when ignoring child ordering)

The number of distinct reachable strings equals the product over all nodes of:
- (number of children)! / (product of factorials of counts of isomorphic subtrees)

This is because at each node, we can permute children, but permutations of isomorphic subtrees give the same result.

Algorithm:
1. Parse the parenthesis sequence into a tree structure
2. For each subtree, compute its canonical form (sorted tuple of children's canonical forms) and the count of distinct strings in its orbit
3. The answer is computed recursively using the formula above
