At $x = 2/3 mj$, $L_2(2/3 mj) = -4 mj + 7 mj = 3 mj$.
$L_1(2/3 mj) = -2/3 mj + 5 mj = 4.33 mj$.
Wait! $L_1(2/3 mj) = 4.33 mj > 3 mj$.
So $L_1$ IS dominant at $x = 2/3 mj$!
Why did my intersection logic say $L_1$ is never dominant?
Let's re-evaluate intersection of $L_3$ and $L_1$:
$-12x + 11 mj = -x + 5 mj \implies 11x = 6 mj \implies x = 6/11 mj \approx 0.545 mj$.
For $x < 6/11 mj$, $L_3 > L_1$.
For $x > 6/11 mj$, $L_1 > L_3$.
Intersection of $L_1$ and $L_2$:
$-x + 5 mj = -6x + 7 mj \implies 5x = 2 mj \implies x = 2/5 mj = 0.4 mj$.
For $x < 0.4 mj$, $L_2 > L_1$.
For $x > 0.4 mj$, $L_1 > L_2$.
So:
For $x < 0.4 mj$: $L_3$ is max (since $L_3 > L_2 > L_1$).
For $0.4 mj < x < 6/11 mj$: $L_3$ is still max (since $L_3 > L_1$ and $L_3 > L_2$).
For $x > 6/11 mj$: $L_1 > L_3$. Also $L_1 > L_2$ (since $x > 0.4 mj$).
What about $L_0$?
Intersection of $L_1$ and $L_0$: $x = 5 mj$.
For $x < 5 mj$, $L_1 > L_0$.
So the upper hull is: $L_3$ for $x < 6/11 mj$, then $L_1$ for $x > 6/11 mj$.
Let's check $x = 1 mj$.
$L_3(1 mj) = -12 mj + 11 mj = -1 mj$.
$L_1(1 mj) = -1 mj + 5 mj = 4 mj$.
$L_0(1 mj) = 0$.
Max is $L_1(1 mj) = 4 mj$.
So the dominant lines are $L_3$ and $L_1$.
Wait, my previous ratio logic was for ADJACENT lines.
For adjacent lines, $L_i$ and $L_{i+1}$, the intersection is $x = mj \frac{skill[i+1]}{skill[i]}$.
If this intersection is not increasing, it means $L_{i+1}$ dominates $L_i$ BEFORE $L_i$ dominates $L_{i-1}$.
This means $L_i$ is NEVER dominant over its neighbors!
So we can pop $L_i$ from the stack!
This is EXACTLY the standard convex hull trick algorithm!
Since the intersections of adjacent lines are $x_i = mj \frac{skill[i+1]}{skill[i]}$, we just need to maintain a stack where $x_i$ is strictly increasing!
Let's build the stack for skill = [1, 5, 2, 4].
$x_0 = 5/1 = 5$.
$x_1 = 2/5 = 0.4$.
Since $0.4 < 5$, we pop $L_1$.
Now stack has $L_0$. We check intersection of $L_2$ and $L_0$.
Wait, the intersection of $L_2$ and $L_0$ is NOT $x_1$.
The standard CHT pops $L_1$ and then checks intersection of $L_2$ and $L_0$.
Intersection of $L_2$ and $L_0$:
$-6x + 7 mj = 0 \implies x = 7/6 mj \approx 1.16 mj$.
So $x_{0, 2} = 7/6 mj$.
Next, $L_3$. $x_2 = 4/2 = 2$.
Since $2 > 7/6$, we keep $L_2$.
So the hull is $L_0, L_2, L_3$.
Wait, earlier I found the true hull is $L_3, L_1$.
Let's re-evaluate!
$L_0(x) = 0$
$L_2(x) = -6x + 7 mj$
$L_3(x) = -12x + 11 mj$
Intersection of $L_3$ and $L_2$: $x = 2/3 mj \approx 0.66 mj$.
Intersection of $L_2$ and $L_0$: $x = 7/6 mj \approx 1.16 mj$.
Since $0.66 < 1.16$, the hull $L_0, L_2, L_3$ is valid!
Let's check the max at $x = 1 mj$:
$L_3(1 mj) = -1 mj$.
$L_2(1 mj) = 1 mj$.
$L_0(1 mj) = 0$.
Max is $L_2(1 mj) = 1 mj$.
But earlier I said $L_1(1 mj) = 4 mj$!
Why is $L_1$ not in the hull $L_0, L_2, L_3$?
Because $L_1$ is ABOVE $L_2$ at $x = 1 mj$!
Wait, if $L_1$ is above $L_2$, then the hull $L_0, L_2, L_3$ is WRONG!
Why did CHT pop $L_1$?
Because intersection of $L_1$ and $L_0$ is $5 mj$.
Intersection of $L_2$ and $L_1$ is $0.4 mj$.
Since $0.4 < 5$, CHT pops $L_1$.
But this assumes $L_1$ is NEVER dominant!
However, $L_1$ IS dominant at $x = 1 mj$!
Let's check $L_1(1 mj) = 4 mj$.
$L_2(1 mj) = 1 mj$.
$L_0(1 mj) = 0$.
$L_3(1 mj) = -1 mj$.
So $L_1$ gives the absolute maximum at $x = 1 mj$!
Why did CHT pop it?
Because CHT requires slopes to be monotonic and we want to find the UPPER hull.
Wait, the slopes are $0, -1, -6, -12$. They are strictly decreasing.
For upper hull, we need the intersection points to be strictly increasing.
Intersection of $L_1$ and $L_0$ is $5 mj$.
Intersection of $L_2$ and $L_1$ is $0.4 mj$.
Since $0.4 < 5$, the sequence of intersection points is NOT increasing.
This means $L_1$ is BELOW $L_2$ for $x < 0.4 mj$, and BELOW $L_0$ for $x > 5 mj$.
But what about $0.4 mj < x < 5 mj$?
In this range, $L_1$ is ABOVE $L_2$ and ABOVE $L_0$!
So $L_1$ IS dominant in the range $(0.4 mj, 5 mj)$!
If we pop $L_1$, we lose the maximum in this range!
Ah! Standard CHT pops a line if it is NEVER dominant.
But $L_1$ IS dominant in $(0.4 mj, 5 mj)$.
Wait, if $L_1$ is dominant, why does CHT pop it?
Let's draw it.
$L_0$: passes through $(0, 0)$ and $(5 mj, 0)$ wait, $L_0(x) = 0$.
$L_1$: passes through $(0, 5 mj)$ and $(5 mj, 0)$.
$L_2$: passes through $(0, 7 mj)$ and $(7/6 mj, 0)$.
At $x=0$, $L_2 > L_1 > L_0$.
At $x=0.4 mj$, $L_2 = L_1 = 4.6 mj$.
At $x=1 mj$, $L_1 = 4 mj$, $L_2 = 1 mj$, $L_0 = 0$.
So $L_1$ is indeed the maximum at $x=1 mj$.
Why did CHT pop $L_1$?
Because CHT assumes that if $x_{1, 2} < x_{0, 1}$, then $L_1$ is below the maximum of $L_0$ and $L_2$.
Let's check if $L_1$ is below $\max(L_0, L_2)$.
At $x=1 mj$, $\max(L_0, L_2) = \max(0, 1) = 1$.
But $L_1(1 mj) = 4$.
So $L_1$ is NOT below $\max(L_0, L_2)$!
Wait! This means the lines DO NOT FORM A CONVEX HULL!
Why? Because the slopes are decreasing, but the intercepts are arbitrary!
CHT ONLY works if the lines form a convex hull, which means the intersection points must be increasing.
If intersection points are not increasing, popping $L_1$ is WRONG because $L_1$ can be above the max of $L_0$ and $L_2$.
Let's verify this.
$L_1$ is a line with slope $-1$. $L_0$ has slope $0$, $L_2$ has slope $-6$.
Since $L_1$ has an intercept of $5 mj$, and $L_2$ has an intercept of $7 mj$, $L_2$ starts higher but drops much faster.
So $L_1$ crosses $L_2$ from below at $x = 0.4 mj$.
Then $L_1$ crosses $L_0$ from above at $x = 5 mj$.
So $L_1$ is above BOTH $L_0$ and $L_2$ in the interval $(0.4 mj, 5 mj)$.
This is a CONCAVE dip in the upper envelope!
Therefore, we CANNOT use CHT! We must keep all lines!
Wow. This is a crucial observation. CHT is invalid here because the intercepts $mj \times spref[i+1]$ do not guarantee convexity.
The intercepts are $mj \times spref[i+1]$, which are increasing, but they don't increase fast enough to maintain convexity.
So we CANNOT optimize the max query with CHT. We MUST evaluate all $n$ lines.

Let's reconsider the $O(nm)$ approach.
$n, m \le 5000$.
Inner loop is just:
```python
max_s = 0
for i in range(n):
    val = F[i] - mj * spref[i]
    if val > max_s:
        max_s = val
```
This is $n$ subtractions and comparisons.
Then:
```python
for i in range(n):
    F[i] = max_s + mj * spref[i+1]
```
This is $n$ additions and multiplications.
Total operations: $2 \times 5000 \times 5000 = 50,000,000$.
In Python, 50M simple arithmetic ops take about 1-2 seconds.
Can we optimize it?
Yes, we can use list comprehensions or `max` with a generator, or `numpy`.
Since we are writing a standard LeetCode solution, `numpy` might not be allowed or might be slow to import.
Let's optimize the pure Python loop.
```python
class Solution:
    def minTime(self, skill: List[int], mana: List[int]) -> int:
        n = len(skill)
        m = len(mana)
        spref = [0] * (n + 1)
        for i in range(n):
            spref[i + 1] = spref[i] + skill[i]
        
        F = [0] * n
        for j in range(m):
            mj = mana[j]
            # max_s = max(F[i] - mj * spref[i] for i in range(n))
            # Let's unroll or use local variables for speed
            max_s = -float('inf')
            s = spref
            f = F
            for i in range(n):
                val = f[i] - mj * s[i]
                if val > max_s:
                    max_s = val
            
            for i in range(n):
                f[i] = max_s + mj * s[i+1]
                
        return F[n-1]
```
Wait, is `max_s` initially 0 or `-inf`?
For $j=0$, $F=[0,0,0,0]$, so `max_s` will be 0. So 0 is fine.

Let's do a quick dry run of the code structure.
```python
class Solution:
    def minTime(self, skill: List[int], mana: List[int]) -> int:
        n = len(skill)
        spref = [0] * (n + 1)
        for i in range(n):
            spref[i + 1] = spref[i] + skill[i]
        
        F = [0] * n
        for mj in mana:
            max_s = max(f - mj * s for f, s in zip(F, spref))
            F = [max_s + mj * s for s in spref[1:]]
            
        return F[-1]
```
This is incredibly short and elegant.

Let's consider if we can maintain `max_s` without recomputing from scratch?
Since $F$ changes completely at each step, we can't easily maintain `max_s` incrementally.
Wait, let's look at the new $F$:
$F_{new}[i] = S_j + mj \times spref[i+1]$.
The next step's `max_s` is:
$\max_i (F_{new}[i] - m_{j+1} \times spref[i])$
$= \max_i (S_j + mj \times spref[i+1] - m_{j+1} \times spref[i])$
$= S_j + \max_i (mj \times spref[i+1] - m_{j+1} \times spref[i])$.
This is exactly what I analyzed before!
The term inside the max is $mj \times spref[i+1] - m_{j+1} \times spref[i]$.
This is a set of lines in terms of $m_{j+1}$:
$y = -spref[i] \times m_{j+1} + mj \times spref[i+1]$.
Since $spref[i]$ is strictly increasing, the slopes are strictly decreasing.
This is exactly the Convex Hull Trick!
Wait, earlier I showed that CHT might pop a line that is dominant.

The slopes are $-spref[i]$, which are strictly decreasing.
If slopes are strictly decreasing AND intercepts are strictly increasing, does it form a convex hull?
Let's check!
Slope $m_1 < m_2$. Intercept $b_1 < b_2$.
Intersection $x = \frac{b_2 - b_1}{m_1 - m_2}$.
Since $b_2 > b_1$ and $m_1 < m_2$, the numerator is positive and denominator is negative!

Numerator is $mj \times spref[i+2] - mj \times spref[i+1] = mj \times skill[i+2] > 0$.
So $x = \frac{mj \times skill[i+2]}{skill[i+1]}$.
Since both are positive, $x > 0$.
This means any two adjacent lines intersect at $x > 0$.
Does the sequence of intersection points $x_i$ increase?
$x_i = mj \frac{skill[i+2]}{skill[i+1]}$.
This is proportional to the ratio of adjacent skills!
If the skills are $1, 5, 2, 4$, the ratios are $5/1 = 5$, $2/5 = 0.4$, $4/2 = 2$.
The intersection points are $5 mj, 0.4 mj, 2 mj$.
These are NOT increasing!
When the intersection points are not increasing, the upper envelope is CONCAVE!
If the envelope is concave, CHT pops lines that are NOT dominant on the convex hull, but ARE dominant on the concave envelope!
Wait, if the envelope is concave, CHT is WRONG because it assumes convexity and removes the "dips".
But we want the MAXIMUM of the lines.
If the envelope is concave, the maximum is exactly at one of the dips!
So CHT removes the exact lines we need!
Therefore, we CANNOT use CHT. We MUST evaluate all lines.
My previous conclusion was 100% correct. CHT is invalid here.
