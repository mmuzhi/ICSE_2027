Let's re-verify the proof.
The objective function for a pair `(i, j)` is `f(i, j) = min( min(i+1, n-i) + min(j+1, n-j), max(i+1, j+1), max(n-i, n-j) )`.
Let `S_min` be the set of indices of `min_val`, and `S_max` be the set of indices of `max_val`.
We want to find `min_{i \in S_min, j \in S_max} f(i, j)`.
Notice that `f(i, j)` is a minimum of three terms:
1. `f_1(i, j) = min(i+1, n-i) + min(j+1, n-j)`
2. `f_2(i, j) = max(i+1, j+1)`
3. `f_3(i, j) = max(n-i, n-j)`

To minimize `f_2(i, j)`, we want `i` and `j` to be as small as possible. So `i = min(S_min)` and `j = min(S_max)`. This is exactly the pair `(min_L, max_L)`.

To minimize `f_3(i, j)`, we want `n-i` and `n-j` to be as small as possible, which means `i` and `j` as large as possible. So `i = max(S_min)` and `j = max(S_max)`. This is exactly the pair `(min_R, max_R)`.

To minimize `f_1(i, j)`, we want `i` to be close to one end and `j` to be close to the other end.
The values of `i` that are closest to the ends are `min_L` (closest to left) and `min_R` (closest to right).
The values of `j` that are closest to the ends are `max_L` (closest to left) and `max_R` (closest to right).
So the pair that minimizes `f_1` must be either `(min_L, max_R)` or `(min_R, max_L)`.

Since `f(i, j) = min(f_1(i, j), f_2(i, j), f_3(i, j))`, the minimum over all pairs must be achieved by one of the pairs that minimizes `f_1`, `f_2`, or `f_3`.
Therefore, the optimal pair is among `{(min_L, max_L), (min_R, max_R), (min_L, max_R), (min_R, max_L)}`.
The proof is solid.
